♞ How to Play Bughouse 2v2

Bughouse is team chess: four players, two boards, one shared fate. You and your partner play opposite colors on adjacent boards, and every piece you capture goes to your partner's hand to be dropped back into their game. This site implements bughouse with a few house rules of its own, explained below.

The basics

  • Teams. Team 1 plays White on board A and Black on board B; Team 2 plays the remaining two seats. You always see your own board on the left, from your side of the table.
  • Captures cross over. When you capture a piece, it appears in your teammate's reserve on the other board. Because your teammate plays the color you are capturing, the piece arrives ready for them to use.
  • Drops. On your turn you may either make a normal chess move or place a piece from your reserve on any empty square (with the restrictions below). Click a reserve piece or drag it onto the board — a ghost preview shows where it will land.
  • Winning. The first checkmate or flag fall on either board ends the match for both teams. There is no coming back from your partner's loss, so watch their board too!

House rules

  • No drop may give check. Unlike classic bughouse, you cannot place a piece so that it attacks the enemy king. Drops are for defense and development — checks must be earned with moves.
  • Pawn drop limits. A pawn may not be dropped on your relative 7th or 8th rank (ranks 1–2 from Black's side), nor on your own back rank.
  • Promotion takes a piece from the other board. When your pawn reaches the last rank, you don't conjure a new queen — you remove a knight, bishop, rook or queen of your own color from the partner board (it belongs to the opposing team's player there), and your pawn becomes that piece. A piece whose removal would leave a king in check over there — a pinned piece — cannot be taken. If nothing can be taken, the pawn simply cannot promote yet.
  • Promoted pieces are temporary. When a promoted piece is captured, it reverts to a pawn in the capturer's team's hand, as in classic bughouse.
  • Sitting is legal. If you are in check with no move, but a future drop could block the check, you are not mated — you may wait for your partner to capture the piece you need while your clock runs. Only unstoppable checks (contact and knight checks with no escape) are checkmate.

Controls

  • Moving: click a piece then its destination, or drag and drop. Legal squares are highlighted; the hovered square glows during a drag.
  • Dropping: click or drag a piece from your reserve. A translucent ghost previews the placement.
  • Premove: while your opponent thinks, you may queue one move or drop (shown in purple). It plays instantly when your turn arrives, or is cancelled if it became illegal. Right-click your board to cancel it. A premoved promotion takes the strongest piece available.
  • Asking for pieces: the "Ask for" buttons send your teammate a pulsing notification, and the matching enemy pieces glow on their board so they know what to hunt.

Clocks and time

Every player has an independent clock and both boards run at the same time. Pick a preset from 1+0 bullet up to 10+0 rapid, or set custom minutes and increment. The increment is added after each of your moves. In online games the server keeps the official time, so laggy connections can't bend the clock.

A little strategy

  • Talk to your partner. Bughouse is a conversation. Use the piece-request buttons: a knight in hand at the right moment is worth more than a rook tomorrow.
  • Trades are never neutral. Every capture you make arms your partner — and every piece you lose arms your opponent's partner. A "fair" trade on your board can be a disaster on the other one.
  • Mind the no-drop-check rule. Since drops can't give check, mating attacks need normal moves to deliver the final blow. Drops shine as blockers — keep a pawn in hand to plug a check before it becomes mate.
  • Promotion is a weapon against the other board. Pushing a pawn to the 8th doesn't just upgrade your army, it physically removes an enemy piece from your partner's game. A far advanced pawn forces the opposing team to keep their pieces pinned defensively — sometimes literally.
  • Watch both clocks. If your opponent is low on time, slowing down on your own board can win the match next door.

Playing online

Click Create game, share the 6-character code, and have your friends join and pick seats. The match starts when all four seats are taken. If someone disconnects their seat is held and they reconnect automatically with the full position restored. After a game, any player can start a rematch.
